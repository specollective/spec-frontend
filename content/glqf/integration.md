## Definition

Integration is the process of reflecting on, synthesizing and combining knowledge in order to make sense of, unify and share information within collaborative and evolving environments.

## Role within the GLQF

Integration is one of three overarching constructs — alongside Knowledge and Engagement — that together distinguish college- and university-level learning from knowledge or skill alone. The GLQF asks learners, faculty, and evaluators to look for evidence that knowledge is being synthesized across contexts, not just retained in isolation.

Many qualification frameworks studied during the development of the GLQF use the overarching constructs of *Knowledge*, *Skills*, and *Abilities*. The GLQF authors found that knowledge, skills, and abilities can exist at any educational level, from primary school through doctoral work. To signal that learning is specifically at the undergraduate level, the framework requires Engagement and Integration alongside knowledge.

## How Integration shows up across the eight domains

Integration is expected to bridge all eight learning domains. A learner with deep specialized knowledge who cannot relate, apply, or recombine it across situations is demonstrating knowledge without integration. Integration is most directly visible in the **Integrated Knowledge** domain, but evaluators look for it everywhere — in how a learner connects specialized concepts to applied practice, weaves ethical considerations into decisions, and reflects on their own learning process.

## What to look for as evidence

- Synthesis across multiple sources or experiences, not just within one course or job
- New connections being drawn between fields, disciplines, or contexts
- Reflection that produces shifts in perspective or strategy
- Work products (essays, designs, projects) that combine and reframe ideas rather than reproduce them
- Collaboration that visibly incorporates the perspectives of others into the learner's own thinking
